Characters

The five 3D meshes the engine keeps resident across every field scene — Vahn, Noa, Gala, and two smaller auxiliary actors — straight from your own disc image. The active party also runs an equipment-conditional mesh swap: a single per-character byte in the save record flips the visible weapon-bearing group between two pre-baked descriptors. 87 player-ANM bundles ship across the corpus, one per scene. Each record is one animation clip (a varying-length frame table over a small set of animated bones; `marker_1 = 0x080C` opens every record body, with size obeying 16 + 8 × bone_count × frame_count byte-exact across the 296-record corpus). Pick a bundle + record, then play to apply the per-frame per-bone transforms to the field-form TMD. Each 8-byte (bone, frame) entry is read as 4 little-endian i16s; the working hypothesis treats the first three as (rx, ry, rz) in PSX 12-bit fixed-point (4096 = 360°), applied as a delta from frame 0 about each object's vertex centroid. The exact semantic of the four shorts is the open thread — frame-0-as-rest-pose makes the animation visible regardless of the underlying scaling. How it works

PROT entry 0874 (befect_data) carries a five-TMD pack at the head of its first LZS section. The engine installs every body at DAT_8007C018[0..=4] at scene entry — a contiguous global pool the field renderer, the actor allocators, and the world-map dispatcher all read. The same five meshes are reused across every town and dungeon (and the world map), so they're loaded once and survive every transition.

Slots 0/1/2 are the active-party characters (Vahn / Noa / Gala). Each ships with twelve group descriptors on disc, but the engine immediately caps the live group_count to ten — the last two are equipment-conditional templates. Every frame, FUN_8001EBEC reads a single byte from each character's save record and overwrites one visible group with either template. That single bit is the entire equipment-swap mechanism for the mesh; weapon identity is conveyed through the texture atlas, not by adding new geometry.

The equipment swap

The toggle in the viewer mirrors what the engine does at runtime: it copies one of two pre-baked 28-byte group descriptors over a visible group in the TMD. Group 10 (TMD+0x124) is the variant the engine picks for a non-zero equipment byte; group 11 (TMD+0x140) is the variant for a zero byte. The patched-group index is character-specific — Vahn patches group 0, Noa patches group 3, Gala patches group 5 — because each character has its primary visible-equipment object in a different mesh slot.

asset character-pack … --slot 0 --equip 1 --out vahn_equipped.tmd

The battle form

When the engine enters BattleMode (game_mode = 0x15), the same DAT_8007C018[0..=4] table the field renderer reads is repointed at a different five-TMD archive at PROT 1204 (other5). The battle-form meshes are higher-fidelity (typical disc-form nobj 15/16/15 vs. the field form's 12/12/12), and the pack carries its own seven 256x256 4bpp character TIM atlases at fixed stride 0x8224, with CLUTs at VRAM rows (0, 490..495, 497).

The battle form is delivered as a flat streaming-format container with asset type 0x09 (TMD2) chunks — distinct from the field form's parse_player_lzs-shaped pack. Slot identity is pinned by byte-equality against the live DAT_8007C018[0..=2] allocation in the party_battle_gobu_gobu save state. The 7 atlases are 256x256 4bpp TIMs with 256-color sub-CLUTs (the character face / hair / weapon textures the renderer samples).

What's next

Three open threads, in priority order of what changes the picture above:

  • Field-form texture atlas. Lives in player_data (PROT 0876, a streaming-format file pinned resident alongside the mesh pack). The streaming TIM_LIST chunk uses a non-canonical head (count=0); the canonical pack-walker can't unpack it yet. Without it, the field form renders flat-shaded — the disc vertices ARE in model-space (byte-equal vs live DAT_8007C018 allocation in the catalogued save state) but the low-poly geometry looks like floating planes without textures.
  • Battle-form texture atlas. Wired. The seven trailing TIMs in PROT 1204 upload into the same 1024×512 paletted VRAM the world-overview viewer uses; the fragment shader does the per-prim CBA / TSB lookup the retail GPU does. Gala renders fully assembled (yellow hair, red shirt, fists raised); Noa renders with her tall hair piece reading as a separate shape (which it is in the source TMD — the design has the hair as a distinct mesh object); Vahn's stored pose has the weapon arm extended along +X, so the AABB is wide and the body sits offset from frame center. Orbit / zoom to inspect.
  • Animations. Live in the ANM container and feed through the per-actor anim record at actor[+0x4C]; the disc-side source is still open. The disc TMD itself stores a single static pose (the engine's chosen default frame); to switch poses or play arts / spells, per-object transforms have to come from the ANM stream — that's the unblocking step for an animated preview of each art with audio.
Note on the disjoint look. The character TMDs ship already in model-space (no per-object rest-pose transform stack to apply). What looks "broken" — Vahn shrunken in frame, Noa with a floating hair piece — is the combination of (a) the static pose baked into the disc bytes, (b) flat-shading without textures (field form only), and (c) the model's AABB asymmetry when a weapon or limb extends past the body. The bounding sphere the camera frames against has been switched to a vertex-centroid sphere (instead of AABB-midpoint) so the body anchors the camera regardless of pose asymmetry.

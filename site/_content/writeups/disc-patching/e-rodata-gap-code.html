Tier E - code injection via the rodata gap

The first tier where you stop editing the game's data and start adding its behavior. Some changes can't be expressed as a byte flip - a second equipment drop the single drop slot physically can't hold, an experience reward the escape path never reaches. Those need new instructions: hand-assembled MIPS parked in a reclaimed all-zero region of the executable, reached by detouring an existing routine. This is where the technique stops being “patch a value” and becomes “splice in code.”

Tier E overview
Tier E of the A–F ladder - the first code-injection tier. The next rung, tier F, exists precisely to escape this tier's one limitation: the gap is finite and shared. New here? Start at the series index.

The reversing: a gap to host it, a seam to enter it

Code injection needs two finds, and both are reversing problems before they're engineering ones.

A reclaimable host. The executable carries runs of zero bytes in its rodata segment that nothing references - padding between sections. One such ~1 KB run (around 0x8007AB38–0x8007AF40) is the routines' home. Reclaiming it safely means confirming it is genuinely unreferenced and genuinely zero, and then guarding every write as all-zero so an already-patched or atypical image is rejected, not corrupted.

A seam at the right moment. The detour has to land in control flow at exactly the point where the new behavior belongs, with no live register clobbered across the jump. Finding it means tracing the relevant routine in Ghidra to the precise instruction pair to displace, then preserving those two instructions inside the routine so the original semantics survive. The detour itself is the standard idiom - j to the routine, do the work, replay the displaced pair, j back - but it only works because the hook site and the displaced instructions were pinned exactly.

Worked examples

Bonus equipment drops - the slot can't hold two

A monster record has exactly one drop slot, so “also drop a rare piece of gear” cannot be a data edit - there is nowhere to put the second item. The detour goes into the battle-end reward routine (FUN_8004E568) at 0x8004F610, displacing two instructions; the gap routine at 0x8007AB80 rolls the per-battle chance, picks an equipment id from a small table appended right after it in the gap, grants it on top of the normal drop, replays the displaced pair, and returns to 0x8004F618. The normal drop is never disturbed.

Run-away EXP - the path that grants nothing

The battle-action escape teardown (the state-machine FUN_801E295C, state 0x66) tears the fight down without ever reaching an experience grant, so rewarding a successful flee needs code. The detour sits at 0x801E5A10 - inside the battle overlay - and routes to a routine in the always-resident SCUS gap at 0x8007AD00 that banks a configured percentage of the fled formation's EXP into the party. The split is deliberate: the hook lives in the overlay (reloaded each battle), but the routine lives in SCUS so it survives every overlay swap.

Enemy ally - charm an enemy instead of adding one

“Have an enemy fight for you” sounds like adding a party member, but retail battles are hard-wired to three party slots (the setup loop FUN_800513F0 is bounded < 3, and party meshes / HUD only exist for slots 0–2), so a genuine fourth combatant isn't possible. The trick is to reuse a state the game already has: Confuse/Charm. The action SM flips an actor's target to the opposite side when its +0x16E & 0x380 “AI-delegated” bits are set - the mechanism behind a confused party member attacking allies. Set the same bits on an enemy at battle setup and that enemy attacks the other enemies. So the gap routine is tiny: it just rolls the per-battle chance and OR-s 0x380 into the frontmost enemy's flags. The detour to it sits at 0x80051990 (in SCUS, right after the monster-setup loop, so the actor table is populated) and the routine lives at 0x8007ACA0 - the free window between the bonus-drop and flee-EXP routines.

It needs one companion edit outside the gap. The monster-wipe victory check counts a monster as “down” only if it is dead or non-targetable (andi v0,v0,0x4 at 0x801E6638 in the battle overlay); a charmed-but-alive enemy would otherwise be one you still had to defeat. Widening that mask to 0x384 (a one-instruction edit) makes a 0x380-charmed enemy also count as down, so the battle ends once the real enemies fall. On a solo-enemy boss the lone enemy simply turns on itself.

Shiny Seru - a persistent bonus with nowhere to store it

A “shiny” capturable enemy (2% per battle) is two problems at once. The first is easy: at battle setup, roll the chance and, if the frontmost enemy is capturable, multiply its stat block ×135/100 and stamp a free per-actor byte (+0x226) as a shiny marker. There's no clean “is a Seru” field on retail (the actor+0x3e byte an early pass guessed is volatile - it reads non-Seru values like 0x55 for a Gobu), so the capturable set is built at patch time from the disc's own monster names - every enemy named after a player Seru-magic (Gimard, Theeder, …) - baked into a 256-bit allowlist bitmap that the setup routine indexes by the first-monster id. (Subtle but vital: every injected routine has to honour the R3000 load-delay slot - a value you just loaded isn't in the register until one instruction later. The stat-boost loop reading lhu then immediately multu silently multiplied the previous field instead, turning the ×1.35 into a runaway cascade until a nop/reorder fixed it.)

The second is the interesting one: the +35% damage bonus must persist after you capture the Seru, across save/load, forever. Retail's character record has no spare per-Seru field - a captured Seru is just an (id, level) pair. The first design leaned on a clever-looking trick: the level byte is never above 9, so its high bit 0x80 is free; OR it in at capture and the flag rides inside the level byte for free - through the spell-list insertion shift (each capture slides the list down) and through a memory-card save. It works for the gameplay readers, as long as every reader masks it: the damage scaler, the level-up < 9 gate, the level-up write-back, the menu digit. But that “mask it everywhere” assumption is exactly where overloading a live field bites back. The spell level-up + display routine (FUN_800402f4, shared by the post-battle results, the magic menu, and battle) reads the level unmasked and does both a level < 9 cap and a (level-1) table index. With the bit set the level reads 0x81/0x82 (129/130): the < 9 test fails so the “grew to level N” box renders blank. Masking that function too didn't fit the remaining gaps, and it's a whack-a-mole anyway - so the fix was to stop overloading the level byte at all.

The flag moved out of the level byte into a parallel per-spell-slot shiny-byte array in a reclaimed-free run of the character record. Now the level byte is always clean - the display reads correct levels, no masking needed anywhere - and the four level-mask routines simply disappear (the patch got smaller). The price is one new routine: because the array is indexed by slot, a grant-shift hook mirrors the game's insert-at-front spell-list shift onto the shiny array so each Seru keeps its flag as the list slides. The capture path writes a clean level plus the shiny byte; one scratch word links “this enemy was shiny” to the grant. That fixed the blank level-up box. The lesson is the technique's own caution sign: a free bit in a live field is free only if you control every reader - and on a sealed disc you usually don't.

The same trap, one level down: “zero” is not “dead”

A separate symptom shipped alongside that first version: during the victory pose, a character's mouth texture was garbled (green when open, scrambled when closed). It was first lumped in with the level-byte bug - the out-of-bounds (level-1) index looked like a plausible cause. But it survived the refactor above untouched, which was the tell: it was never the level byte. The actual cause is the gap-code technique biting its own hand. The routines themselves had been dropped into “reference-free all-zero” runs that were really the zero padding of two live, indexed tables: the victory mouth-override table (0x80077E80, addressed rows from 0x800781B0 - the per-character facial keyframes the victory animator plays) and the battle overlay's move-power table (0x801F4F5C). Those tables have rows/records that are all-zero for unused entries, but the game still indexes them at runtime. So the victory face animator dutifully read our routine bytes as facial keyframes (the garbled mouth), and six attack ids whose move-power records landed in the cave read our code as power + trail-texpage data (wrong damage, garbage effect textures). The assert_zero guard had passed - the bytes genuinely were zero in the clean ROM - which is exactly why “is it zero?” is necessary but not sufficient.

The fix relocates every routine and datum to runs verified three ways: all-zero in the clean image, constant-zero across diverse in-battle save states (which catches runtime scratch buffers - e.g. a name buffer that's zero in the file but holds “Vahn” at runtime), and outside every known table - a structural guard (assert_not_in_tables over the catalogued SCUS / overlay table ranges) that refuses any region overlapping a live table even if it reads as zero.

The trap a third time - and the only check that actually closes it

That guard was confidently described as making the bug “impossible to recur.” It recurred. The relocation moved three of the routines/data into fresh zero runs in the 0x80079xxx region - which turned out to be the zero padding inside the SsAPI sound/effect tables. The guard didn't catch it for the dullest possible reason: those tables weren't in the denylist, because nobody had mapped them yet. The symptom was specific and baffling: the game froze only when you used an item in the tutorial fight - a Healing Leaf would announce, the HP would refill, and then the battle just… spun, forever, taking no input. No patched code was even running at the freeze (the menu/draw hooks were inert with their flag clear). The cause was data, not code: the item-use sound engine indexes a table at 0x800794F0, and a few entries past its real end it walked straight into our capturable-Seru bitmap. It read our bytes as sound parameters, the item's sound never reported “done,” and the sound-synced item banner waited on it forever.

The lesson sharpens: a denylist of “known tables” is only ever as complete as your map of the binary, and on a sealed disc that map is never complete. The authoritative test isn't “is this address in a table I know about?” - it's “does any instruction read this address at runtime?” So the final placements were chosen by putting a read-watchpoint on each candidate run in a live emulator and exercising the worst cases - an item use, a victory pose, a summon cast - keeping only the runs that no instruction ever touched. Shiny's nine routines now live in four such read-watch-verified regions (0x80077728 gap 1, 0x8007AE00 arena 1 up to the sound table at 0x8007AF00, 0x8007AFF8 arena 2, 0x80078A88 slot 6), and the “+35% DMG!” caption sits one line below the native effect box instead of overlapping it. It mirrors the clean-room engine, where the same feature is just a HashSet of (character, spell) in seru_learning and a ×1.35 in the damage kernel - the gap-code version is what that idea costs on a sealed disc, including the cost of being wrong, three times, about which bytes are free.

The contended resource

The three routines, the unused-item name string, the engine config blob, and an overlay-loader stub all live in the same ~1 KB run. Each tenant guards its slot as all-zero, so they compose - but the region is finite, and that ceiling is exactly why a whole interactive screen goes somewhere else.

Mods using this technique

  • Bonus equipment drops (--equipment-drops) - an additive drop the one record slot can't encode.
  • Run-away EXP (--flee-exp) - a reward the escape path never grants on its own.
  • Enemy ally (charm) (--enemy-ally) - flips an existing enemy onto your side by setting its Confuse/Charm bits at battle setup; no static table flags a charmed enemy. Pairs the gap routine with a one-word victory-check widen in the battle overlay.

Adding a tier-E mod: confirm a reclaimable all-zero gap (and that it fits your routine), find a hook site at the right control-flow moment, preserve the displaced instructions, guard every write as dead space, and add a disc-gated oracle. If your routine is screen-sized, you've outgrown the gap - go to tier F.

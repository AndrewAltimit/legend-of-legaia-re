Sol Fever Disco — Noa's dance

Hit the step on the beat. The notes are the game's own step chart, baked into the dance overlay; the groove gauge is the difficulty lane — every closed step chain fills it, and each thousand promotes you to a denser row of the chart worth more points. Triangle is the wildcard: three “groovy moves” a song, good on any beat, but only worth the big multiplier when you spend one on a 4-beat combo slot — and it spins Noa for up to a second, during which nothing you press is judged. So save them for the end of your longest combos. The two dancers beside you are running the same chart for real: beat them.

Retail splits the chart lookup in two: the hit judge matches your press against the raw chart cell, while the display half substitutes the held-sequence symbol on every fourth beat. The lane-0 chart puts every note on a fourth beat, so the notes above are the cells the judge actually scores — the ones you can hit. Auto-play off the displayed cell and you score zero.

The chart's symbols aren't abstract notes: FUN_801d4040 maps each one straight to a pad-button mask, so a symbol is a button — 1 = Square (0x80), 2 = Circle (0x20), 3 = Triangle (0x10), the three-times-only “groovy move” wildcard.

The Triangle is not a direction. Its branch in the award routine (FUN_801d1af4, pad 0x10) matches no chart cell at all: it gates on a stock counter (DAT_801d534c, set to 3 at run start and never refilled), scores (lane+1) × 3 wherever you spend it but (lane+1) × 0x19 when it lands on the 4-beat combo slot — where it also steps the groove gauge a full +1000, promoting the lane — and then throws the dancer into a lane + 1 turn spin (DAT_801d564c) during which the actor handler stops calling the judge at all. That is the input disruption. The game's own how-to script says it in words: press it together with the last command of a combo, and you only get three.

Baka Fighter — the duel

Rock-paper-scissors with life meters, best of three. 2 beats 1, 3 beats 2, 1 beats 3. Your opponent throws from a scripted pattern on the disc, so it can be read. Take a hit and your combo counter feeds the next hit you take — get behind and it snowballs. Drop under a sliver of HP and a comeback critical can fire. The Special is unbeatable but slow: it wins the exchange the moment they commit, and the whole round if you let it charge to full first.

Start a run and the cabinet opens on its PLAYER SELECT screen — the three party fighters' battle models under the sheet's own banner; arrow keys (or a click) pick one, Enter confirms. Then climb the ladder: each stage is best-of-three, and after every win the retail tally menu asks NEXT GAME or PAY OUT. Fight on and the accumulated prize pot rides on the next match — lose and it's forfeit. Pay out and it banks. Clear all fourteen rungs and the whole pot pays, 460 coins from rung one. "Start at" lets you drop in at any rung.

The fighters are named on the disc. Each roster record opens with a 32-byte ASCII name at +0x00 — in the bytes just ahead of the stat block, which is why a parser that starts at the stats never sees them. The names above are read off your image, not a lookup table here.

The ladder order is the disc's too, and it resolves an oddity: read the roster straight down and the prize gold looks jumbled. It isn't. The cabinet seeds its stage counter to 2 and picks roster = stage + 3, so the twelve rungs it actually serves are records 5–16 — across which the prize is strictly increasing, and sums to — coins. Records 3 and 4 pay out of turn because you only meet them on the second lap, after the all-clear wraps the counter to 0. They're the post-clear opponents the victory screen promises when it says the game isn't over yet.

The duel above is drawn with the cabinet's own assets, decoded from your disc at runtime. The fighters are the real 3D meshes — your side from the battle-form party pack (PROT 1204, the same models the turn-based battles use), the opponent from its own per-rung pack (PROT 1206…1219: a [TIM][TMD][anim] chain, one per ladder fighter) — posed every frame from their real animation banks (the party’s from the PROT 1203 battle-form bank, the opponent’s from its own pack; idle loops, the exchange winner’s swing / loser’s knockdown play as they land, and the match winner throws a short victory flourish from the same attack slots). The arena is the stage TMD out of PROT 1203’s second descriptor: the tall patterned backdrop wall with its lattice fences and ceiling lamps, standing at its authored base on the fighters’ floor plane. The HUD — the VITAL bars with their traced gradient (FUN_801d2afc: right-anchored at x 0x89, one pixel per 32 HP), the round-win pips, the combo counter, the attack-icon columns, the ROUND / FIGHT! / YOU WIN! banners, the PLAYER SELECT screen’s banner + cursor arrows, and the between-match NEXT GAME / PAY OUT / GET COIN tally menu — comes from the overlay’s own 51-record widget table (DAT_801d7160, read by the quad emitter FUN_801d5ed0) over the 9-TIM art pack in PROT 1203, byte-checked against a retail VRAM dump.

The cash-out is the retail mechanic. The art pack’s tally sheet carries the whole menu as widget cells — NEXT GAME, PAY OUT, GET COIN with its own coin-digit strip, and the VICTORY! / ALL STAGE CLEAR! / IT’S NOT OVER YET. block — so the after-every-win choice is the cabinet’s own. Two rules here are stated readings rather than overlay-pinned constants: a mid-run loss forfeits the whole pot, and the final rung pays out automatically. The per-rung prizes are the roster records’ own gold column (the strictly-increasing ladder that sums to the 460-coin full clear).

Traced vs fitted, stated plainly. Every sprite cell, palette and HUD screen position above is read off the disc or the disassembly. The 3D camera, the fighters’ floor spacing, and the tally-menu / select-screen layout are fitted by eye — the duel’s GTE matrices live in COP2 and no mid-duel capture exists yet (the parked save state sits at the title screen). The stage set carries no floor mesh, so the floor is tiled from the backdrop wall’s own dominant textured face — its exact texture cell and palette, repeated; the tiling itself is a fit, and the wall’s three prop meshes (which need untraced placement transforms) stay out rather than being guessed. The HP-bar frame cells come from a runtime-built table (DAT_801dbc34) a static page can’t read, so a plain outline stands in; the attack/knockdown slot order within each anim bank (and so the victory flourish’s swings) is inferred from the action-id fold in FUN_801d3f44; the coin-digit strip’s u = 88 + digit×16 stepping is inferred from the sheet layout the way the traced digit widgets step; and the minigame’s sound stays silent here because its cues are static-bank ids resolved through SCUS_942.54’s descriptor table, which this PROT-only page doesn’t load. When the presentation assets don’t decode, the duel falls back to the stat panel above and says so — no stand-in art.

Vidna casino — slot machine

Three coins a spin, all five paylines always live — three straight and two diagonal. Stop each reel yourself. The machine keeps a hidden net-take counter — the more it has taken off you, the better the odds it rolls — and a big bonus win knocks that counter back down. Land three blue kicks and you earn 1 bonus round; three red punches, 3. A bonus round rotates the reels onto the machine’s other strip — the numerals 1–10, one row per frame as the reels turn — and the marquee across the top counts your stops: 0 x 0 x 0, filling in each column as you claim it. Stop all three and it pays their product, 1 to 1000 coins. Nothing steers those reels; the numbers are your timing.

Space is the whole machine. Press it to spin, then press it once more per reel to stop them left to right — three presses, three reels, which is exactly what the cabinet's three stop buttons do. A win banks itself, so there is nothing to collect; when the coins run out, a press racks a fresh machine. Clicking the machine counts as a press, for want of a spacebar.

Internals (reverse-engineered)

The tables above are the player-facing prize / roster data. Each minigame's runtime is reverse-engineered too - its per-frame state machine, scoring math, and data tables, traced from the captured overlay code:

  • Fishing - a reel tug-of-war against a tension gauge, with the 10 fish species driven by a per-species parameter table decoded from the disc. The point-exchange counter is decoded too: two per-venue 6-row prize tables ([limit, price, item id]) whose row 0 hides until strictly affordable - which is how Buma's 20,000-point Water Egg "appears", and how a hidden 50,000-point War God Icon at Vidna stayed off the walkthrough prize lists. One-time prizes latch a persistent bitmask; the same venue select pages the rod-by-cast-band species-spawn table.
  • Slot machine - a reel state machine with two random-number generators (one for the reels, one for the feature odds, which bracket on a hidden net-take counter: the machine gets more generous the more it has taken from you, and big bonus wins knock the counter back down) and a per-symbol payout table; your per-spin balance only commits to the shared casino coin bank when you cash out at the counter - which is why an "Infinite Coins" cheat reads full at the cage but doesn't make spins free.
  • Baka Fighter - rock-paper-scissors, best of 3, against 17 scripted opponents each with their own prize and attack pattern.
  • Dance - a beat clock with an accuracy-weighted timing judge, a baked step chart selected by difficulty, and per-tier score multipliers.
  • Muscle Dome - a card battle: deal a hand, commit cards into the fighter's action queue under a point budget, and resolve through the game's regular battle engine (it lives inside the battle code, not its own minigame module).

All five are playable in the clean-room engine - and three of them (dance, Baka Fighter, the slot machine) run right here in the browser, off the same rules engines compiled to WebAssembly. The two that don't are the ones whose rules aren't self-contained: fishing pulls its species table from the fishing overlay but its venue and rod state come from the save file, and the Muscle Dome resolves its committed cards through the full turn-based battle engine rather than any minigame code of its own. In the engine's play-window, dance (K), fishing (L), the slot machine (O), Baka Fighter (B), and the Muscle Dome (M) all run as suspending scene modes driven by ported rules engines that decode their tables from your disc at runtime. In Baka Fighter you counter the opponent's scripted pattern in a best-of-3 duel (a match win banks the ladder opponent's gold prize); in the Muscle Dome you commit strike-command cards under a point budget - the cards' costs are the same per-weapon bytes that size the battle Arts gauge. While fishing, P opens the decoded point-exchange counter: caught-fish points bank into a persistent pool and trade for the real per-venue prize rows.

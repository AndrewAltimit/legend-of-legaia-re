Coin-economy minigames, prize-exchange counters, and the side-quest chains layered through Sol Tower (CDNAME town0d). Baka Fighter — the duel

Rock-paper-scissors with life meters, best of three. 2 beats 1, 3 beats 2, 1 beats 3. Your opponent throws from a scripted pattern on the disc, so it can be read. Take a hit and your combo counter feeds the next hit you take — get behind and it snowballs. Drop under a sliver of HP and a comeback critical can fire. The Special is unbeatable but slow: it wins the exchange the moment they commit, and the whole round if you let it charge to full first.

Vidna casino — slot machine

Three coins a spin, all three paylines always live. Stop each reel yourself. The machine keeps a hidden net-take counter — the more it has taken off you, the better the odds it rolls — and a big bonus win knocks that counter back down. Land a line of the two jackpot symbols and the bonus round opens: cheap spins that pay the product of the line.

Internals (reverse-engineered)

The tables above are the player-facing prize / roster data. Each minigame's runtime is reverse-engineered too - its per-frame state machine, scoring math, and data tables, traced from the captured overlay code:

  • Fishing - a reel tug-of-war against a tension gauge, with the 10 fish species driven by a per-species parameter table decoded from the disc. The point-exchange counter is decoded too: two per-venue 6-row prize tables ([limit, price, item id]) whose row 0 hides until strictly affordable - which is how Buma's 20,000-point Water Egg "appears", and how a hidden 50,000-point War God Icon at Vidna stayed off the walkthrough prize lists. One-time prizes latch a persistent bitmask; the same venue select pages the rod-by-cast-band species-spawn table.
  • Slot machine - a reel state machine with two random-number generators (one for the reels, one for the feature odds, which bracket on a hidden net-take counter: the machine gets more generous the more it has taken from you, and big bonus wins knock the counter back down) and a per-symbol payout table; your per-spin balance only commits to the shared casino coin bank when you cash out at the counter - which is why an "Infinite Coins" cheat reads full at the cage but doesn't make spins free.
  • Baka Fighter - rock-paper-scissors, best of 3, against 17 scripted opponents each with their own prize and attack pattern.
  • Dance - a beat clock with an accuracy-weighted timing judge, a baked step chart selected by difficulty, and per-tier score multipliers.
  • Muscle Dome - a card battle: deal a hand, commit cards into the fighter's action queue under a point budget, and resolve through the game's regular battle engine (it lives inside the battle code, not its own minigame module).

All five are playable in the clean-room engine - and three of them (dance, Baka Fighter, the slot machine) run right here in the browser, off the same rules engines compiled to WebAssembly. The two that don't are the ones whose rules aren't self-contained: fishing pulls its species table from the fishing overlay but its venue and rod state come from the save file, and the Muscle Dome resolves its committed cards through the full turn-based battle engine rather than any minigame code of its own. In the engine's play-window, dance (K), fishing (L), the slot machine (O), Baka Fighter (B), and the Muscle Dome (M) all run as suspending scene modes driven by ported rules engines that decode their tables from your disc at runtime. In Baka Fighter you counter the opponent's scripted pattern in a best-of-3 duel (a match win banks the ladder opponent's gold prize); in the Muscle Dome you commit strike-command cards under a point budget - the cards' costs are the same per-weapon bytes that size the battle Arts gauge. While fishing, P opens the decoded point-exchange counter: caught-fish points bank into a persistent pool and trade for the real per-venue prize rows.

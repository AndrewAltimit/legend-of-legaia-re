Enemy table

Decode every enemy's stat record and 3D battle model straight from your own disc image. The full archive - names, HP, MP, combat stats, and the embedded mesh for the whole roster - is LZS-decompressed in your browser via WebAssembly. Click any row to spin its model. No game data ships with this site; bring your own .bin and nothing leaves your machine.

Reading the columns

ATK
The enemy's physical offense.
UDF / LDF
Upper and lower defense - two facets; which one applies depends on the attack.
INT
Governs magical damage and defense against magic, and also seeds the hit / evade roll - the column fan bestiaries call INT.
SPD
Sets battle turn order.
AGL
The agility / action gauge the AI spends to act each round (the in-game "Power Up" buff raises it - "agility increased!").
Elem
The monster's element. The affinity scale nudges an attacker's damage by the (attacker element, defender element) pair - a small ±4% effect in retail: same-element 96%, opposite-element 104%, otherwise 100%.
EXP / Gold / Drop
The victory spoils - base values the game scales and splits at the end of a fight.
Steal
What the player takes with the Evil God Icon equipped (item + chance). Steals come from a separate table in the executable, not from the monster record - which is why a monster's steal and drop usually differ.
Magic attacks
The enemy's named spells and their MP cost.

Names for spells, drops, and steals resolve from a full disc image; load only a raw PROT.DAT and the raw id shows instead (the steal table lives in the executable, so a raw PROT.DAT shows no steal).

International release stats vs. the raw record

The four combat columns (ATK, UDF, LDF, INT) default to the in-battle values - what the player actually fights - because the raw on-disc record is not what gets installed into the enemy. When the battle loader copies a record into the live actor it boosts those four stats: ATK ×5/4, UDF ×2, LDF ×2, INT ×9/8. HP, MP, AGL and SPD are copied unchanged. Untick Show international release stats in the toolbar to see the raw record bytes instead.

This boost is why retail enemies hit harder than the raw record (or older bestiaries built from it) suggest - a difference across regions first surfaced by Zetopheonix. The loader carries two boost profiles picked by a per-battle flag; the table shows the profile that a live international-retail capture reproduces byte-for-byte. EXP and gold are a separate story: the record holds a pre-reduction base that the victory-spoils routine scales down (EXP ×¾ then split among the party; gold roughly ×¼-⅓) at the end of a fight - the EXP / Gold columns show those base values.

How it works

The whole roster lives in one archive on the disc. Each enemy id maps to a fixed slot that LZS-decompresses to a stat record followed by the enemy's 3D model and its animations. The same parser the native tools and the engine use runs here, compiled to WebAssembly - so what you see is exactly what the game reads off the disc. The combat columns then apply the battle loader's stat boost so the numbers match what the player fights (see the note above); untick Show international release stats for the raw record bytes.

Files are read with the browser's FileReader straight into WebAssembly memory. Nothing is uploaded; the project ships zero Sony bytes (bring your own disc). Your disc is cached locally and shared across pages, so it auto-loads on reload - use Forget to clear.

asset monster-archive extracted/PROT/0867_battle_data.BIN

The 3D model

Click any row to spin its battle model - rendered textured and directional-lit straight from your disc, the same model the battle camera shows. 186 of the 194 enemies carry one (the rest are filler ids).

asset monster-archive … --id N --obj enemy.obj --texture-png enemy.png

Animations

The model isn't static. Every action an enemy can play - its idle resting loop, its move cycle (the approach it plays advancing on a target - a walk for grounded enemies, a flight cycle for fliers), and each spell / special - ships as its own transform-keyframe stream in the same archive. The viewer opens on the idle loop; when a monster carries more than one animation, a row of toggle buttons appears beside the model so you can switch between them. Each one re-poses the body parts frame to frame exactly as the battle renderer does.

asset monster-archive … --id N --anim
Reference Monster animation

Download

The ⬇ Download .glb button packs the selected enemy - mesh, baked texture, and all of its action animations - into a single binary glTF file. glTF is the universal 3D interchange format, so the model drops straight into Blender, three.js, the Windows 3D Viewer, or any glTF tool, animations and all. Each TMD body part becomes an animated node; the per-part palettes are baked into one texture atlas. The same export is available offline from the CLI.

asset monster-archive … --id N --glb enemy.glb

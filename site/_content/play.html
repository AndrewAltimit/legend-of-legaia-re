Play the port

The clean-room engine, running in your browser, on your own disc. This is not a video and it is not an emulator: it is the actual Rust port compiled to WebAssembly - the field VM, the free-movement controller with its per-scene walkability grid, the floor-height sampler, the NPC motion VMs, the interaction probe - stepping once per frame while WebGL draws the world it is simulating. Pick a disc, pick a place, and walk around inside it. Nothing is uploaded; the image never leaves your machine.

Scenes

What actually works

This is a partial reimplementation and the honest map matters more than a demo reel. What runs in the browser today:

  • Field and town scenes. The map assembles from the scene's environment mesh pack, its .MAP object grid, the floor-height table, and the walk-ground heightfield, textured from the real PSX VRAM the engine builds. You walk with the ported free-movement controller: the same 2-unit stepping, the same per-axis collision against the per-scene walkability grid, the same terrain follow.
  • The player character. The lead's field mesh out of the global TMD pool, posed every frame from the real locomotion clips - idle while standing, the walk cycle while moving.
  • NPCs. Every actor the scene's MAN places, on its own animation clip, at the position the engine's motion VMs put it. Walk up to one and press Z: the interaction probe runs and the field VM plays out their dialogue, branches and all.
  • Doors. Walk into one and the engine loads the destination scene under you; the page rebuilds the geometry around the transition.

What does not run here yet, and why:

  • Battles. The battle state machine and its formulas are ported and tested, but the battle stage's render path lives in the native window. Random encounters are therefore left disarmed in the browser rather than dropping you into a black screen.
  • The title screen and the opening prologue. The boot chain, the narration crawl, and the cutscene camera all run natively; their draw path is the native one. "New game" here starts you at the scene the disc's new-game table names, not at the title card.
  • The pause menu, shops, and the inn. The runtime state is all there; the menu's window-descriptor draw path has not been ported to the WebGL renderer.
  • Audio. The sequencer and the SPU are ported and the WebAudio backend exists, but the BGM director is not wired into this page yet.
  • The overworld. World-map scenes load, but their locomotion and camera are a different controller; the world overview is the place to see the continents today.

Some scenes frame better than others, and the reason is a real gap rather than a page bug. Retail authors a camera per scene; this page runs one generic follow camera, so it culls whatever straddles the line from the lens to the player - that is what keeps a cave roof or a monastery ceiling from filling the screen. Caves also expose a deeper disagreement: for scenes whose floor is not in the floor-height table, the engine's floor sampler reports height 0 while the drawn walk-ground sits below it, so the character can appear to stand above or inside the rock. That is an engine-level thread, tracked with the others, not a mystery.

How this page is wired

The WebAssembly bundle is the engine crates themselves - engine-core (world state, scene host, VMs) and the asset parsers - compiled for wasm32-unknown-unknown. The page owns three things and no more: it turns key state into a PSX pad word, it calls tick_frame() once per animation frame, and it draws what the engine reports. The engine decides where you are; the page never moves the player itself.

Character meshes ship their vertices in object-local space - each limb's coordinates are relative to its own joint - so every actor on screen is assembled per frame as R·v + T from the animation record's bone transforms. Draw one without that and you get a heap of parts at the origin. See character meshes and ANM.
